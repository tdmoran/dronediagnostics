# SLIDE 2: The Problem

## The Shifting Standard of Care

- AI recommendations are becoming part of the **documented clinical standard**
- Ignoring AI without justification carries malpractice risk
- No specific case law exists — yet
- The physician who overrides without rationale assumes AI's liability

### The Key Question:
> *"What happens when clinical judgment contradicts algorithmic recommendations?"*

---

# SLIDE 3: The Liability Spectrum

## Three Scenarios

| Scenario | Risk Level |
|----------|-----------|
| **Override without reason → patient harmed** | 🔴 HIGH malpractice risk |
| **Override with documented reasoning → patient harmed** | 🟡 GRAY AREA — courts examine justification |
| **Override questionable AI → patient benefits** | 🟢 DEFENSIBLE — upheld duty of care |

# SLIDE 4: FDA Framework (US)

## Clinical Decision Support Software (CDSS)

- **Exempt** from premarket review if not replacing clinician judgment
- **Override recording mandatory** — systems must log physician overrides
- **Rationale requirement** — must be "clear and justifiable"
- Post-market surveillance tracks override patterns
- Device labeling: "Does not replace physician judgment"

---

# SLIDE 5: EU AI Act

## High-Risk Medical Devices

### Human Oversight Mandate
- Healthcare professionals **must** be involved in all decisions
- Clinician override **explicitly protected**

### Liability Structure
- Device failure → **Manufacturer liable**
- Clinician misuse → **Professional liable**

### Requirements
- All decisions must be **traceable** and **explainable**

# SLIDE 6: WHO Guidance

## Core Principles

1. **"Human in the Loop"**
   - AI supports, never replaces, human professionals

2. **Clinician Override Protected**
   - Necessary for patient safety

3. **Documentation Requirements**
   - Transparency
   - Explainability
   - Documented rationale

---

# SLIDE 7: The Documentation Framework

## Required Elements for Override Documentation

1. **AI RECOMMENDATION** — What the system suggested
2. **OVERRIDE DECISION** — Alternative action taken
3. **CLINICAL JUSTIFICATION** — Patient-specific factors
4. **ALTERNATIVE ACTION** — Detailed description
5. **PEER CONSULTATION** — Specialist input (if any)
6. **RISK-BENEFIT ANALYSIS** — Why benefits outweigh risks
7. **OUTCOME TRACKING** — Patient response

---

# SLIDE 8: Strengthening Factors

## What Strengthens Override Justification?

✅ Patient-specific factors (allergies, genetics, comorbidities)  
✅ Clinical intuition based on physical exam  
✅ Recent clinical developments  
✅ Patient preferences after informed consent  
✅ Specialist consultation  
✅ Documented AI limitations  

---

# SLIDE 9: Weakening Factors

## What Weakens Override Justification?

❌ "Gut feeling" without clinical basis  
❌ Contradicting AI without reviewing reasoning  
❌ Convenience or cost alone  
❌ No documentation of rationale  
❌ Preventable harm from ignored AI recommendation  

---

# SLIDE 10: Explainability

## Explainable AI (XAI) Requirements

- AI must provide reasoning for recommendations
- Doctors need to understand **WHY** before overriding
- "Black box" AI creates documentation challenges

### Key Questions:
1. What data points drove the recommendation?
2. What populations was the AI trained on?
3. What are the AI's known limitations?
4. Is there new evidence the AI hasn't incorporated?

# SLIDE 12: Surgical Considerations

## Unique Factors in Surgery

- **Real-time decisions** require rapid but documented justification
- **Haptic feedback** may contradict imaging AI
- **Dynamic anatomy** invalidates pre-op AI predictions
- **Team communication** — entire team must understand override

# SLIDE 14: The Fundamental Principle

## Key Takeaway

> **"The physician who overrides AI without documented justification assumes the liability that the AI system would have otherwise borne."**

### The AI-Clinician Partnership
- AI creates a **documented baseline**
- Override decisions become **medicolegal events**
- Documentation is **imperative**

# SLIDE 15: Recommendations

## For Individual Clinicians

1. Treat AI as specialist consultation — review carefully
2. Document every override with patient-specific rationale
3. Understand AI limitations for your specialty
4. Maintain traditional clinical skills

## For Institutions

1. Develop clear AI override policies
2. Implement EHR workflows requiring justification
3. Provide AI explainability training
4. Create peer review processes

---

# SLIDE 16: Conclusion

## Summary

The integration of AI in medical decision-making creates a **new category of clinical-documentation liability**.

- Physicians retain **ultimate decision-making authority**
- Exercising that authority requires **explicit, documented justification**
- The future belongs to clinicians who can **augment** AI capabilities while maintaining **accountability**
